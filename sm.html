The Layers of the Earth

The layers of the Earth are the distinct regions of the planet that differ in composition, structure, and properties. Each layer plays an important role in the overall functioning of the Earth, from providing a foundation for life to influencing geological activity.

Inner Core

The inner core is the Earth's innermost layer. It is solid and extremely hot, primarily composed of iron and nickel. It is under immense pressure, making it solid despite the high temperatures.

Outer Core

The outer core is a liquid layer composed mainly of iron and nickel. It lies beneath the mantle and is responsible for the Earth's magnetic field.

Mantle

The mantle lies beneath the Earth's crust. It is composed of hot, semi-solid rock that moves slowly over time. It is the largest layer of the Earth and is responsible for the movement of tectonic plates.

Crust

The Earth's crust is the outermost layer. It is solid and relatively thin compared to the other layers. The crust is divided into tectonic plates that float on the semi-fluid layer beneath.
